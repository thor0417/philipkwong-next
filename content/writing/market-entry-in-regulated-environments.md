The conventional wisdom on ASEAN market entry is that the region is complex, relationships matter more than process, and patience is a prerequisite. All of this is true. It is also incomplete. The organizations that succeed in regulated ASEAN markets are not just the patient ones. They're the ones that understand what "regulated" actually means in each jurisdiction—and those meanings differ considerably across Thailand, Vietnam, the Philippines, Indonesia, and the rest of the region.

## What Founders Get Wrong

The most common mistake I see from founders entering regulated ASEAN markets is applying a single model across multiple jurisdictions. The ASEAN Economic Community has created real integration at the trade level, but regulatory frameworks remain deeply national. What is required in Thailand may not be recognized in Vietnam. A compliance architecture built for the Philippines may not transfer to Indonesia without significant modification.

The second mistake is sequencing. Many founders treat regulatory compliance as something to address after market validation—a cleanup task once product-market fit is established. In regulated environments, this sequencing is backward. Regulatory positioning determines what you're allowed to offer, to whom, and through what channels. Market validation done outside the regulatory perimeter produces data about a market you may not be permitted to operate in.

The third mistake is conflating government relations with regulatory compliance. These are related but distinct capabilities. Government relations is about relationships and positioning—being known to the right people and understood correctly. Regulatory compliance is about documentation, process, and operational architecture. Both are necessary. Having one without the other is the most reliable predictor of a stalled market entry.

## Thailand as a Case

Thailand offers a useful illustration because it sits at an unusual intersection: a well-developed regulatory apparatus, significant international investment interest, an active government agenda around specific industries, and a political environment that requires careful navigation.

For companies in regulated sectors—healthcare, cannabis and related industries, food safety, financial services—Thailand's regulatory bodies are professional and process-driven. The challenge is not that the regulators are difficult to work with. The challenge is that the process takes time, the documentation requirements are specific, and the informal guidance that helps you navigate the process is not written anywhere.

The organizations that move fastest through Thai regulatory processes are not the ones with the best lawyers. They're the ones that arrive at the first meeting having already done the work—with documentation that anticipates the regulator's concerns rather than responding to them. This requires knowing what those concerns are before you arrive. It requires relationship investment before you need it, not during the process.

## Vietnam and the Evolving Framework

Vietnam presents a different challenge. The regulatory framework for most sectors is evolving rapidly, often in ways that aren't fully reflected in the published regulations. What the law says and what the regulator actually requires can diverge significantly, and the divergence isn't always predictable.

For market entry into Vietnam, the due diligence question isn't only "what does the regulation permit?" It's "what is the enforcement direction?" A permissive regulation that is moving toward stricter enforcement creates different risks than a restrictive regulation that is being selectively applied. Understanding the enforcement trajectory requires local knowledge that doesn't come from reading the statute.

## The Dual-Base Advantage

Operating between Vancouver and Bangkok provides a specific kind of advantage for regulated ASEAN market entry. The Canadian regulatory experience—particularly in sectors like cannabis, quality management, and technical standards—is directly relevant to the questions ASEAN regulators are working through as they build their own frameworks.

ISO IWA 37-1 drew participants from 22 countries, many of them in Asia, specifically because the international experience of other markets was seen as directly applicable to local framework development. Organizations that can translate Canadian regulatory architecture into ASEAN regulatory context have a genuine positioning advantage—not as foreign experts imposing models, but as participants who understand both the technical requirements and the local context well enough to be useful to both sides.

## The Principles That Transfer

Regardless of jurisdiction, regulated ASEAN market entry works best when it follows a few consistent principles.

Lead with the regulatory architecture, not the product. Define what compliance looks like in this jurisdiction for this product before you build your go-to-market strategy. The market you can legally address may be different from the market you think you're entering.

Invest in relationships before you need them. Regulatory relationships in ASEAN are built on demonstrated understanding and long-term intent. They are not available on demand during a compliance crisis. The meetings you have when nothing is urgent are the meetings that matter when something is.

Build compliance as a local capability, not a foreign import. A QMS developed in Canada and translated into Thai is not a Thai QMS. The most durable compliance architectures are built with local operators who understand local conditions, anchored to international frameworks that provide structure and credibility.

The organizations that establish durable positions in regulated ASEAN markets are not the ones that moved fastest. They're the ones that moved correctly—with regulatory architecture in place before they needed it, relationships built before they became critical, and a local operational capability that works within the environment rather than against it.
